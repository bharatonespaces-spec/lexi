Domestic Violence Act: Your Right to Residence

December 14, 2024 | Women's Rights

One of the biggest fears women face when reporting domestic abuse is the fear of homelessness. "Where will I go?" is a question that silences many. The Protection of Women from Domestic Violence Act (PWDVA), 2005, answers this powerfully with the "Right to Residence."

What is a Shared Household?

The law defines a "Shared Household" as a house where the woman lives or has lived in a domestic relationship. It does not matter if the house is owned by the husband, rented, or belongs to the in-laws (in certain contexts). A woman cannot be evicted from this house without following the due process of law.

Residence Orders (Section 19)

If you are facing domestic violence, a Magistrate can pass a Residence Order that:

  • Restrains the abuser from dispossessing you or throwing you out of the shared household.
  • Directs the abuser to remove himself from the shared household (conditions apply).
  • Restrains the abuser or his relatives from entering the portion of the household where you live.
  • Directs the abuser to secure the same level of alternative accommodation for you or pay rent for the same.

Economic Abuse is Violence Too

Domestic violence is not just physical hitting. If you are denied money to run the household, forced to leave your job, or your stridhan (assets) are being withheld, that is Economic Abuse under the Act. You are entitled to monetary relief.

Emergency Steps: If you are in immediate danger, dial 100 or the Women's Helpline 1091. You can then approach a Protection Officer or a lawyer to file a Domestic Incident Report (DIR).

Conclusion

The law is designed to ensure that a woman does not have to choose between her safety and a roof over her head. You have the legal right to live in your matrimonial home with dignity.
